# Loom Design Review — Adversarial Self-Critique

**Status:** Design review · 2026-07-07 · reviewer: principal engineer (red-team mandate)

## 1. Review scope & method

This is a hostile read of the complete Loom design doc-set as of 2026-07-07: the architecture overview; all twelve ADRs; the seven platform docs (`host-agent`, `isolation`, `control-plane`, `networking`, `security`, `agent-protocol`, `renter-api`); the six ML-lifecycle docs (`data`, `training`, `evaluation`, `serving`, `environments`, `recipes`); and the four product docs (`deployment`, `marketplace`, `roadmap`, `unit-economics`). I read for the failure modes a serious team wants named *before* build, not discovered by users. The docs are unusually honest — they flag most of their own soft spots as "open questions." That honesty is precisely the problem this review attacks: **a flagged open question is not a de-risked one**, and several of the flags sit directly on the critical path with no resolution plan and no owner. Where the design's own numbers indict it, I use those numbers rather than inventing new ones. Findings are ranked by severity; every finding carries a concrete resolution path.

## 2. Findings

### F1 — The MVP (Phase 1) generates no operator revenue, and the docs half-admit it. **CRITICAL**

`unit-economics.md §2` computes contribution per rented 4090-hour at **+$0.0072** — dominated entirely by the $0.05/hr support amortization — and the 3090 line at **−$0.0142**. `roadmap.md` Phase 1 is *batch-only, no inference, Tier B containers, 50–100 vetted GPUs*. But `unit-economics.md §5` and §3 are explicit that **inference contribution per GPU dominates rental by ~100×** and that "a 4090-heavy fleet is roughly break-even on rental and needs inference to be the business." Inference does not exist until Phase 2. So Phase 1 — the phase that must prove the flywheel and survive long enough to reach Phase 2 — is by the design's own arithmetic a break-even-at-best batch marketplace whose contribution is a rounding error swamped by a *guessed* support cost. The seed fleet is described as "mostly 4090/5090, few H100" (§6), i.e. the mix that §5 shows clears infra burn "only if it isn't 4090/3090-heavy." The docs never reconcile "seed fleet is 3090/4090/5090-class" (`marketplace.md §6`) with "3090/4090 rental is break-even-to-negative."

Why the current answer is insufficient: the doc treats this as an accepted strategic truth ("batch's job is to fill the fleet, not print money") without confronting the *runway* consequence. A 2–3 engineer team (`roadmap.md`) burning ~$660/mo infra plus headcount, spending 9–12 EM on Phase 1 with contribution near zero, has no self-funding path until Phase 2 lands (another 6–9 EM). That is 15–21 engineer-months before a positive-contribution product exists.

Resolution: **force a decision** — either (a) pull a minimal inference slice into Phase 1 so the fleet earns from day one (contradicts the clean phase boundary but fixes the economics), or (b) accept Phase 1 as pure liquidity-proving and fund it explicitly as a cost center with a named runway, not as a business. Gather the one number that decides it: the real support-cost-per-account (F9), because at $1/account the 4090 contribution is +$0.041/hr (5.6×) and the picture changes materially.

### F2 — gVisor/nvproxy compatibility is unproven on exactly the consumer cards where all early supply lives. **CRITICAL**

`isolation.md §3.3` states plainly that nvproxy officially supports datacenter cards (T4, A100, A10G, L4, H100) and that "the same-architecture *consumer* cards (RTX 30/40-series) 'likely work but aren't officially supported.'" `isolation.md` Open Question #1 asks for "our own per-card qualification matrix." Blackwell/RTX-50-series support is flagged *unverified for 2026 silicon* (OQ #2). Meanwhile: Tier B with gVisor `runsc + nvproxy` is the **default and only** isolation tier at scale (Tier A requires headless/secondary GPU + IOMMU, which a daily-driver gaming rig by definition does not have), and `roadmap.md` risk-2 already ranks this the top technical risk. The entire supply thesis — "someone's gaming rig that also plays games" (`isolation.md §3`) — routes through an unofficial, best-effort GPU-passthrough path on the exact cards nobody has qualified.

Why insufficient: the roadmap's mitigation is "keep non-gVisor Tier B (plain `runc`) as a fallback lane." But `isolation.md §3.1` and §8 are emphatic that **plain `runc` exposes the raw NVIDIA driver ioctl surface and is not an acceptable default** — it is disclosed-weaker isolation, reputation-gated. So the fallback for a security control is *turning the security control off*. If nvproxy breaks common workloads (vLLM, PyTorch, an odd CUDA path) on 40-series cards under a specific driver in the rolling support window, the choice becomes "run insecurely or don't run" — and `nvproxy`'s strict driver-version matching (`runsc nvproxy list-supported-drivers`) means a host on a too-new or too-old driver is simply refused runsc, silently shrinking supply.

Resolution: **the highest-priority pre-build spike (see §4).** Run the 5 curated images end-to-end under `runsc + nvproxy` on 5 real consumer GPUs (3090, 4090, 5090, plus a 4070 and a 3060 for the low end) across the driver-version band the enrollment floor admits. Produce the qualification matrix `isolation.md` already knows it needs. Decide the plain-`runc` policy *from data*, and price the supply loss if the qualified band is narrow.

### F3 — Support-cost-per-account is a GUESS that dominates margin, flips whole GPU classes negative, and has no headcount in the plan. **HIGH**

`unit-economics.md §1` labels `support = $3.00/account/month` a **GUESS**, and §2 shows it is "the dominant cost line" — larger than fees, relay, and storage *combined* by two orders of magnitude on a 4090 hour. §8 lists it as the open question that "flips the 3090 (and price-war 4090) negative" and "blocks any confident statement about batch-tier profitability." Separately, `roadmap.md` sizes the whole company at 2–3 engineers and models on-call as "the founders… $0 incremental cash." **There is no support headcount anywhere in the plan**, yet `unit-economics.md §4` itself notes "consumer nodes die constantly" and disputes/abuse handling is real human load. A marketplace with self-serve host onboarding (`deployment.md`), stolen-card fraud (`marketplace.md §5`), and dispute adjudication that is "manual at v1" (`marketplace.md §4`) will generate tickets.

Why insufficient: the design simultaneously assumes support is (a) cheap enough to guess at $3 and (b) staffed by founders at zero cash. Both cannot hold once the fleet passes a few hundred accounts. If the true number is $6/account, the 5090 line barely clears and the 4090 goes negative even without a price war.

Resolution: instrument disputes/tickets-per-100-jobs from the very first job (`unit-economics.md §7` item 4 already commits to this — make it a Phase 1 exit metric, not a Phase 2 nicety). Model a part-time support role explicitly in the Phase 3 (public launch) headcount. Decide the 3090 policy (F1) as a function of the measured number.

### F4 — Cold-start on CGNAT is minutes-scale and keep-warm may be the *only* viable serverless UX — but keep-warm pricing rests on an unvalidated host-behavior assumption. **HIGH**

`serving.md §4` concludes bluntly: "even the optimized floor is minutes-scale (download-dominated), scale-to-zero is inherently a minutes-scale cold start." A 14B AWQ model is "~3–13 minutes" to pull over residential downlinks; a 32B is worse. The product answer is keep-warm. But `unit-economics.md §3` and `marketplace.md §2` leave the load-bearing question **open**: *does the host get paid during warm-idle seconds?* `unit-economics.md` recommends Branch A (host paid) and states Branch B "is not incentive-compatible unless warm-idle time is short" because "a reserved idle GPU still burns ~350W and blocks the owner's own use." Nobody has asked a real host whether they will pin a 350W card idle on a daily-driver machine at *any* price that also lets the renter's keep-warm floor stay competitive. On a Tier B daily-driver, "keep-warm" means the owner cannot use their GPU *and* is paying electricity — the owner-eject-always-wins guarantee (`host-agent.md §8`, `control-plane.md §4`) directly contradicts a durable warm reservation.

Why insufficient: keep-warm is simultaneously (1) the answer to the #3 roadmap risk (cold-start killing serverless), (2) the margin engine's precondition (billable-util at 60% assumes replicas stay resident), and (3) structurally at odds with the "owner always wins" property on exactly the daily-driver supply that dominates the fleet. The design has not shown that warm capacity can exist on Tier B at all. If keep-warm only works on Tier A / dedicated rigs, the serverless product's supply base is far smaller than the marketplace's.

Resolution: (a) a host-recruitment survey/interview on idle-payout willingness and a concrete warm-rate floor; (b) restrict keep-warm to Tier-A / dedicated-window nodes in the design and re-run the inference economics on that smaller pool; (c) measure real vLLM cold-start on a residential connection (spike in §4) to know how bad scale-to-zero actually is before committing to keep-warm as the sole mitigation.

### F5 — Single-region control plane + NATS is a SPOF under an *always-on* inference product. **HIGH**

`control-plane.md §8` commits to "one region" and §4 to a **single-process scheduler** ("a warm-standby process can take over on crash because leases lapse safely"), with HA explicitly deferred (§10 open question: "Scheduler HA before region sharding… is crash-and-restart acceptable"). The whole control plane is designed to run as "a single Docker Compose… one host box" (§9). This is defensible for a batch marketplace where a control-plane blip means jobs requeue late. It is **not** defensible once Phase 2 ships serverless inference with a live gateway, mid-stream failover, and a keep-warm SLA — the gateway's live replica table is fed by agent heartbeats "routed through the control plane" (`serving.md §3`), and replica placement/autoscaling (`serving.md §6`) is a control-plane maintenance loop. A control-plane region outage or a NATS partition stalls new placements, autoscaling, and replica self-healing for a product whose entire pitch is "always hot."

Why insufficient: `control-plane.md §8` argues the inference *data plane* doesn't hairpin through the control plane (true — token streams go gateway↔node over the relay), so a single region "serves a globally-distributed fleet." That defends *latency*, not *availability*. It says nothing about what happens to the serving fleet's health/placement brain during a single-region failure. NATS is named as the one bus for req/reply, fan-out, and durable streams — a partition degrades all three at once.

Resolution: define a Phase-2 availability target for the gateway/placement path and force the HA decision (`control-plane.md §10`) *before* serverless ships, not after. Decide minimally: warm-standby scheduler with leader election, multi-AZ NATS/JetStream within the region, and a documented degraded-mode (gateway keeps routing to already-warm replicas using its last-known table while the control plane is down). Cheapest first step: a chaos test that kills the scheduler and the NATS leader mid-serving and measures how long placement/failover is blind.

### F6 — Result-integrity spot-checking economics are asserted, never solved, at a 20% take that can't afford redundancy. **HIGH**

`security.md §3.5` frames result integrity as redundant execution at rate *p*: "a rational host that cheats on fraction *c* of its jobs survives an expected 1/(p·c) jobs before ejection," and defers the actual *p* values to `marketplace.md` as "a … economics decision." `security.md §10` and the residual-risk register (R3, R13) leave the sampling rate an **open question**. Now impose the economics: `unit-economics.md §2` shows 4090 batch contribution is **+$0.0072/rented-hr**. Redundant execution at rate *p* costs *p×* extra compute — the operator pays a second host's payout for the duplicate. Even modest *p* (say 5–10%) on a marketplace clipping <1¢/hr obliterates contribution. The design leans on "canaries (near-free per job)" and reserving full duplication for "high-value jobs and new/low-reputation hosts," but for *serverless* streaming, `security.md §4` concedes "per-request streaming makes deterministic byte-matching harder than in batch jobs," pushing serverless onto canaries + "quality plausibility" — i.e. the weakest integrity signal on the highest-value product.

Why insufficient: the whole verification story is "set *p* so cheating is negative-EV" with the stake and *p* both unspecified. At a thin take, either *p* is too low to deter a patient cheater, or it's high enough to erase margin. Canaries only work if a host can't distinguish a canary from a real job — a determined host that fingerprints canary prompts defeats the near-free path, forcing back to expensive duplication.

Resolution: model the integrity budget explicitly — pick a target undetected-bad-work fraction, derive the minimum *p* per reputation band, and check the resulting compute cost against contribution per class (it will only pencil on 5090/H100/inference, not 3090/4090 batch). Prototype canary indistinguishability: can an adversary cluster canaries by prompt distribution? If yes, the near-free coverage assumption fails and the whole *p*-economics need re-deriving.

### F7 — Windows-host exclusion cuts the addressable supply where the seed-fleet population actually lives. **HIGH**

`deployment.md §9` and `roadmap.md` Phase 4 non-goals both exclude Windows hosts: "Linux + NVIDIA only for hosting at GA; Windows hosting is 'much later.'" The supply thesis is "someone's gaming or workstation PC that they also use" (`isolation.md §3`) and the seed fleet is recruited "from the ML/homelab/ex-mining community" (`marketplace.md §6`). The unstated contradiction: **the overwhelming majority of gaming rigs — the daily-driver Tier-B supply the whole model depends on — run Windows.** The isolation stack (gVisor `runsc`, nvproxy, nftables egress, tc/CAKE QoS, VFIO) is Linux-only; `networking.md` OQ #6 explicitly flags "tc/CAKE and nftables are Linux-first. macOS/Windows prosumer hosts need an equivalent shaping + egress-firewall story or a documented 'Linux-host-only' stance." So the marketplace targets gamers but can only accept the small minority of gamers running Linux (plus the ex-mining/homelab niche, which is real but not large).

Why insufficient: the docs treat Windows as a deferrable "marginal supply" (`roadmap.md` punt list) while simultaneously depending on gaming rigs for liquidity. Those are the same machines. Either the addressable seed supply is much smaller than "gaming rigs" implies (mostly homelab/ex-mining Linux boxes), or Windows hosting is not actually deferrable. WSL2 is a renter-CLI path only; it is not a viable untrusted-workload host environment for GPU passthrough + kernel-level egress control.

Resolution: size the *Linux-NVIDIA-with-a-spare-or-idle-GPU* population honestly and check it against the 50–100-GPU seed target and the several-thousand-node scaling claim. If the reachable Linux supply is thin, either (a) commit to a Windows host story earlier than Phase 4 (a real isolation design, not a punt), or (b) rewrite the supply narrative to stop implying gaming rigs and target ex-mining/homelab explicitly. This is a market-sizing spike, cheap and decisive.

### F8 — Identity-stripping is sold as the *primary, structural* renter-from-host protection, but its guarantee is routinely defeated by prompt content. **MEDIUM**

`overview.md` and `security.md §3.2` elevate gateway identity-stripping to the "primary renter-from-host protection… STRUCTURAL, holds even against a fully malicious host." The docs *do* disclose the limit honestly (`security.md §4` limits, R2, R12): the host sees prompt text verbatim; "a prompt that contains 'my name is Jane Doe, SSN …' leaks that PII regardless"; and traffic/timing correlation can re-link scattered requests. My critique is not that the limit is hidden — it's that the *framing* oversells. For the largest real serverless use case (a renter's chatbot relaying end-user conversations — the "end user" principal `security.md §1` says teams forget), prompts are saturated with self-identifying content: names, account references, app-specific system prompts that fingerprint the renter, echoed context. Identity-stripping removes the header `who` while the payload `who` walks straight through. The structural guarantee is real but narrow: it defeats *bulk cross-request correlation by a passive host*, which is a genuine but modest slice of the threat.

Why insufficient: `security.md §8` correctly concludes personal-data workloads belong on Tier C — which doesn't exist until Phase 5. So for Phases 2–4, the honest position is "serverless standard tier is not safe for content-sensitive or personal-data workloads," yet the product (`deployment.md §3c`) markets the 2-minute OpenAI-drop-in path as the top-of-funnel with no such gate. The gap between "structural primary protection" (architecture framing) and "don't put anything sensitive in a prompt until Phase 5" (security reality) is a trust-and-compliance landmine.

Resolution: reconcile the framing — demote identity-stripping from "primary protection" to "correlation-resistance, one layer," and make the content-sensitivity warning a product-surface element (a tier label on the inference path, `security.md §3.4`'s honest labeling actually applied to serverless), not buried in a security doc. Force the decision on whether standard-tier serverless is offered to personal-data renters at all before Tier C ships.

### F9 — Benchmark-fingerprint spec-fraud defense is gameable and the docs say so, but reputation is asked to cover a gap it can't. **MEDIUM**

`host-agent.md §4` is admirably candid: the enrollment fingerprint "buys detection of the cheap lies" (claiming a 4090 while holding a 3060) but "does not buy protection against… one who records genuine benchmark traces and replays them," nor against "a real 4090 [that can] quietly downclock, share the GPU with their own gaming session, or throttle the tenant." It's "a floor on capability at enrollment, not a continuous guarantee." `marketplace.md §4` and `host-agent.md §11` OQ #2 hand the residual to "surprise re-verification + reputation" and the TEE tier — but re-verification "never runs while a tenant job holds the GPU" (`host-agent.md §4`), which is exactly when a host would throttle. So a host that passes enrollment, then shares the card with their own gaming session or downclocks under tenant load, is caught only if (a) a surprise re-bench happens to land in a degraded idle window, or (b) `control-plane.md §6`'s util-vs-fingerprint cross-check fires — and §6/§10 flag that cross-check must "tolerate legitimate thermal throttling… without punishing honest hosts," i.e. it is deliberately blunted.

Why insufficient: the defense catches enrollment-time lies well and runtime under-delivery poorly, on a fleet where runtime under-delivery (owner also gaming, thermal throttle, quiet downclock) is the *common* degradation, not the exotic one. Reputation decays after the fact — the renter already got throttled compute they paid for.

Resolution: add a tenant-side capability check — a lightweight in-sandbox micro-benchmark at job start (and periodically) that the renter's job can assert against the advertised class, feeding disputes with attributable evidence. Decide the threshold with real thermal-throttle data (`control-plane.md §10`) so honest hosts aren't punished. Quantify, as `host-agent.md` OQ #2 asks, the actual cost to a replay/downclock adversary — if it's low, the "verified badge" pricing lever (`marketplace.md §4`) is selling a guarantee that doesn't hold.

### F10 — Residential upload (~30 Mbps) vs. the eject checkpoint window is an unsolved SLA collision for full-model checkpoints. **MEDIUM**

`training.md §3` states the hard case plainly: "a multi-GB checkpoint over ~30Mbps up is **minutes**, which can exceed a tight grace window." `host-agent.md §8` sets the eject checkpoint window default "on the order of a minute" and OQ #6 leaves its tuning open. The collision: full-FT and from-scratch jobs (`training.md §1b,c`) produce multi-GB checkpoints; a 5 GB checkpoint at 30 Mbps is ~22 minutes of upload — 20× a one-minute eject window. `training.md §3` mitigations (incremental shard upload, async overlap, local-first drain with "background drain to object store, so an eject during upload still leaves a resumable state *on a peer if the node reappears*") are real but conditional: local-first drain only helps *if the node reappears*, which on an owner-eject-to-play-a-game is exactly when it won't for hours. `training.md` OQ #1 openly asks whether full-FT needs a minimum-uplink gate or peer-to-peer checkpoint handoff — unresolved.

Why insufficient: the "checkpoint-resume survives a vanishing node" promise (`roadmap.md` Phase 1 exit criterion — *the* core differentiator) is proven for LoRA/QLoRA adapters (tens of MB, "seconds" — genuinely fine) but not for full-model checkpoints, which the design admits are "the worst case" with only partial mitigation. A full-FT job evicted mid-checkpoint on a node that stays gone loses work back to the last completed drain.

Resolution: decide the full-FT policy before Phase 1 exit — either gate full-FT recipes to higher-uplink nodes (`training.md` OQ #1), lengthen the checkpoint interval so eject-window loss is bounded and priced, or build peer-handoff (a nearby node holds the partial checkpoint). Make the Phase 1 checkpoint-resume exit criterion explicitly cover a full-model checkpoint under eject, not just an adapter — otherwise the exit gate proves the easy case and ships the hard one.

### F11 — Legal exposure: operating a marketplace where hosts can demonstrably read renter data — is honest labeling a sufficient legal shield? **MEDIUM**

`security.md §0` and §8 are refreshingly blunt: on consumer cards "in-use encryption of renter data against the machine's owner is physically unavailable," and "Loom cannot honestly sign a data-processing addendum that promises one," so personal-data workloads on Tier A/B are flagged as "for many lawful bases… **not defensible**." R1 (host dumps guest RAM/VRAM) is `Accepted, disclosed` at **High** residual severity. The design's legal posture is "honest labeling + route sensitive workloads to Tier C." The gap: disclosure protects *Loom's honesty*, but it does not by itself resolve *liability*. A renter building on Loom's serverless inference is a GDPR data controller and Loom is a sub-processor two hops from an end user who never chose Loom (`security.md §1` end-user row). If that renter's users' PII lands on a host that dumps it, "we told the renter" may not discharge Loom's sub-processor obligations, and "the renter shouldn't have put PII in the prompt" is a term-of-service argument, not a compliance control. Tier C — the only compliant path — is Phase 5, years out.

Why insufficient: the security doc treats this as a labeling/UX problem (`security.md §8`, "we flag it in-product"). It is also a *contracts and jurisdiction* problem that no doc owns. There is no AUP-enforcement story for "renter processed regulated PII on the consumer tier against our warning," no data-residency handling (hosts are geographically wherever the fleet is; a job can land in any region), and no analysis of whether a US-based operator brokering EU-resident data through unencryptable third-party machines is exposed regardless of labels.

Resolution: get a real legal read before public launch (Phase 3), not before Tier C. Specifically: (a) whether honest labeling + ToS actually discharges sub-processor liability, (b) data-residency controls (can a renter pin jobs to a jurisdiction? the scheduler has `region` but no compliance-region concept), (c) whether standard-tier serverless should contractually prohibit personal data with technical enforcement teeth, not just a warning. This is a spike measured in lawyer-hours, and it can invalidate the "self-serve, no review gate" onboarding (`deployment.md §1`) for whole customer segments.

### F12 — NAT-traversal success rate is assumed from Tailscale's number for a population the docs admit is worse. **MEDIUM**

`networking.md §3` leans on Tailscale's "over 90%" direct-connection figure, then immediately flags: "that figure is Tailscale's estimate for a *basic* implementation, not a controlled measurement of Loom's population. Our population skews *worse*… consumer/prosumer home GPUs sit disproportionately behind CGNAT… plan for a **materially higher relay rate than 10%**." `networking.md` OQ #1 makes "real relay rate for our population" the top open question. This cascades: `unit-economics.md §1` carries `relay_share` at 10/20/30% (base 20%) and `§6b` models a 60% blowout; Dragonfly P2P weight distribution (`networking.md §5`, `serving.md §4`) *assumes peers can reach each other* and "peers behind CGNAT that can't punch will fall back to relay/origin, eroding the savings" (`networking.md §5` flag); and interactive UX (`loom notebook`, `loom ssh`, `deployment.md §4`) rides the same tunnel.

Why insufficient: relay share is a load-bearing input to (a) relay-fleet cost, (b) the P2P weight-distribution savings that make cold-start tolerable, and (c) interactive-session latency — and it is a *guess anchored to a different population's marketing number*. If Loom's real punch rate is, say, 60% direct rather than 90%, the P2P weight mesh degrades toward origin-pull (melting the cold-start budget F4 depends on), relay egress rises, and interactive latency suffers. The docs know this; nobody has measured it.

Resolution: build a tiny NAT-traversal probe app (the cheapest high-value spike in §4) — a two-endpoint hole-punch tester distributed to the recruited seed-host community — and get a real direct-vs-relay distribution *before* Phase 2 commits to Dragonfly-over-WG-mesh and before relay-fleet sizing is fixed. This single measurement de-risks F4, the relay economics, and the P2P weight thesis at once.

## 3. What the design gets right

- **Radical honesty about the hard security truth.** `security.md §0` refuses to ship FHE/white-box theater and states in-use confidentiality is physically impossible on consumer cards — the residual-risk register (R1–R13) is the work of a serious team, not a pitch.
- **Checkpoint/requeue/failover as first-class paths, not afterthoughts.** Treating node disappearance as the *expected* case (`control-plane.md`, `host-agent.md §3`) is the correct architectural spine for consumer supply, and it's consistently applied.
- **The "no Kubernetes" argument is genuinely load-bearing and correct** (`overview.md`, ADR-0004): K8s assumes an owned, inbound-reachable fleet; NAT'd strangers' machines are the opposite. Postgres+NATS+lease-based scheduling fits the actual problem.
- **Curated-image + digest-pinning + driver-floor-at-enrollment** (`environments.md §3`) is a disciplined answer to the CUDA/driver combinatorial swamp, and the Tier-A driver-homogeneity insight (§3.5) is a real, earned advantage.
- **Adopt-don't-build discipline** (`roadmap.md` build-vs-buy ledger): vLLM, Cloud Hypervisor, gVisor, WireGuard, Dragonfly, Stripe — building only the coordination/differentiation layer is the right scope for a small team.

## 5. Verdict

**Buildable, but not as roadmapped without three forced decisions up front.** The architecture is sound where it counts — the consumer-supply constraint is understood, the failure-as-normal spine is right, the security honesty is exemplary, and the scope discipline is real. What the roadmap underplays is that its two riskiest technical bets (nvproxy on unqualified consumer cards, F2; minutes-scale cold-start needing keep-warm on supply that structurally resists it, F4) and its central economic bet (Phase 1 earns nothing and the margin engine arrives a phase late on a support-cost guess that could flip whole GPU classes negative, F1/F3) are all *flagged as open questions and then built past anyway*. A design that names its landmines but schedules the disarming for later is not de-risked; it is documented.

I would say **stop** if any of the following came back badly and the team pressed on regardless: (1) S1 shows nvproxy breaks common workloads across the consumer driver band and the only fallback is insecure plain `runc` — that voids the Tier-B supply thesis; (2) S3+S4 show cold-start is minutes-scale *and* hosts won't hold warm capacity at a viable rate — that voids the serverless product, which is the margin engine; (3) S4+S5 show the reachable Linux-NVIDIA seed supply is too thin to hit 50–100 GPUs *and* support cost flips batch negative — that voids the liquidity flywheel before it spins. Run S1–S4 before writing Phase 1 code. They are cheap, they are the ones that can kill the company, and the design already told us where they are.

## Addendum (2026-07-07, post-pivot)

The refocus to **self-host-first** ([profiles.md](./profiles.md), [ADR-0014](../adr/0014-deployment-profiles-marketplace-optional.md), with the marketplace deferred to roadmap Phase 3) materially changes the exposure of several findings above, because most of them were scored against a marketplace-first MVP that no longer leads. **F1 (Phase-1 generates no operator revenue) is now explicitly accepted, not a risk:** the self-hostable core is a *product the user runs on their own hardware*, not a revenue engine — there is no operator take to be missing, so the "MVP earns nothing" critique dissolves for the core and applies only to the deferred hosted layer. **F7 (Windows-host supply) and F4 (keep-warm pricing rests on an unvalidated host-behavior assumption) move to the deferred marketplace layer** — both are about recruiting and pricing *strangers'* supply, which the self-host profiles don't have; they remain open but are no longer Phase-1 blockers. **F2 (nvproxy/gVisor compatibility on consumer cards) REMAINS critical even for self-hosters:** Tier B is the standalone default isolation tier ([self-host.md](../product/self-host.md) §5), so a self-hoster running a poisoned dependency depends on exactly the same nvproxy compatibility — spike S1 stays a pre-build must-run. The verdict's other technical bets (F3 support cost, F5/F6/F11 marketplace economics and legal) are all marketplace-layer concerns that ride along with the deferral.

An independent external review (Codex) reached consistent conclusions — see [external-review.md](./external-review.md).
